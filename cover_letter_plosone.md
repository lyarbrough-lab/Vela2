# Cover Letter — PLOS ONE Submission
**Manuscript:** Eating the Fire: Biomimetic Boundary Layers and the Case for Radiation Harvesting at Nuclear Interfaces
**Author:** Leslie Yarbrough
**Contact:** vela.yarbrough@gmail.com

Dear PLOS ONE Editorial Board,

I am submitting the manuscript "Eating the Fire: Biomimetic Boundary Layers and the Case for Radiation Harvesting at Nuclear Interfaces" for consideration as a research article.

**What the paper does.** The paper proposes a paradigm shift in nuclear waste management: instead of treating spent fuel radiation as a hazard to be blocked, it presents evidence and simulation that it can be harvested. We draw on three lines of biological precedent — hydrothermal vent metabolisms, radiotrophic fungal melanin (Dadachova et al. 2007; Turick et al. 2011), and cryptochrome-based magnetic transduction in migratory birds (Ritz et al. 2000; Hore & Mouritsen 2016) — to argue that active radiation interfaces are not exotic: they are a repeated evolutionary solution. We then test a specific engineering implementation, the thin-foil graded-Z stack, using a Monte Carlo photon-transport simulation we developed for this purpose.

**Core simulation result.** When total absorber thickness is held constant at 2.6 cm (the fair comparison that prior work missed), charge collection efficiency increases by two to three orders of magnitude as layer thickness decreases from bulk to 5–100 μm. The physical mechanism is simple and robust: secondary electrons produced by gamma absorption in dense metals thermalize as heat before reaching any collection surface in bulk material; thin-foil geometry lets them escape and be collected. (The simulation uses a conservative 10 μm electron range as a lower bound; NIST ESTAR data places the CSDA range of 1 MeV electrons in iron at ~3–4 mm, which would shift the optimal layer thickness toward the millimeter scale — a quantitative correction that does not alter the qualitative finding.) The trend is insensitive to model details because it reflects a fundamental geometric constraint.

**Why PLOS ONE.** This paper sits at the intersection of biophysics, materials science, and nuclear engineering. No single disciplinary journal covers that combination well. PLOS ONE's scope — "scientifically sound research across all areas of science and medicine" — and its emphasis on rigor over perceived impact make it the appropriate venue for this kind of cross-disciplinary, foundational proposal. We have been explicit about the paper's limitations throughout: the simulation is a simplified order-of-magnitude estimate, prior direct-conversion experiments showed rapid degradation, and practical harvesting is not imminent. The claim we make is the one the evidence supports: that thin-foil geometry dramatically improves collection efficiency in principle, and that biology motivates taking the concept seriously.

**Data and code availability.** The Monte Carlo simulator is available in the open GitHub repository referenced in the manuscript (lyarbrough-lab/Vela2). All simulation parameters are reported in Section 8; the results in Table 1 are fully reproducible from the public code.

**Conflicts of interest.** None.

I believe this manuscript will interest readers in biophysics, nuclear materials, and sustainable energy. Thank you for your consideration.
