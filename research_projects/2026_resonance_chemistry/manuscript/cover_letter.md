# Cover Letter — MNRAS Submission

**Date:** June 3, 2026

**To:** The Editor, Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society

**Subject:** Submission of "Stellar-Mass Dependence of Near-Commensurability in Multi-Planet System Architectures"

Dear Editor,

I am pleased to submit the enclosed manuscript, "Stellar-Mass Dependence of Near-Commensurability in Multi-Planet System Architectures," for consideration for publication in Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society.

**Summary of the work:**

We analyze 336 multi-planet systems (1182 planets) from the NASA Exoplanet Archive to investigate whether host star mass influences the resonant architecture of planetary systems. We introduce a stability-filtered null ensemble that separates genuine architectural information from what random packing within stability bounds would produce. Our central finding is a U-shaped dependence of bond fraction on stellar mass: M dwarfs (66%) and F dwarfs (56%) show the highest levels of adjacent-pair commensurability, while G dwarfs (47%) sit at or near the dynamical floor — indistinguishable from stable random packing. This pattern persists under multiple detection filters, alternative null constructions, and varying Hill stability thresholds.

Two candidate mechanisms are proposed: M dwarfs likely produce bonds through extended disk-mediated migration ("slow cooking"), while F dwarfs achieve comparable bond fractions through rapid, vigorous migration ("fast chemistry"). The Solar System — orbiting a G dwarf at the bottom of the U-shape — is largely non-bonded, consistent with this framework.

**Novelty and significance:**

The stability-filtered null methodology is, to our knowledge, a new approach to quantifying architectural information in exoplanet systems. Most existing studies report raw period ratio histograms without accounting for the dynamical constraints on allowed configurations. Our approach reveals that G-dwarf systems are near a dynamical floor where commensurability structure is indistinguishable from random survival — an observational constraint on planet formation models that has not been previously reported.
